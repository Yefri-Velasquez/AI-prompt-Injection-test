How to Protect Yourself from AI Deepfakes

March 30, 2026 · 8 min read

AI-generated deepfakes — synthetic images, audio, and video that convincingly mimic real people — have become increasingly sophisticated. Whether it's a fake video of a public figure or a cloned voice used in a scam call, deepfakes pose real risks to individuals and organizations. Here's how to protect yourself.

What Are Deepfakes?

Deepfakes use deep learning models (typically GANs or diffusion models) to generate or manipulate media. They can:

  • Swap faces in video, placing one person's face onto another's body
  • Clone voices from just a few seconds of audio
  • Generate entire fake people who never existed
  • Manipulate lip movements to make someone appear to say something they didn't

Why Should You Care?

Deepfakes are already being used for:

  • Financial fraud — Scammers clone a CEO's voice to authorize wire transfers
  • Identity theft — Fake video calls used to bypass KYC verification
  • Misinformation — Fabricated videos of politicians or public figures
  • Personal harassment — Non-consensual synthetic media targeting individuals
  • Social engineering — Impersonating trusted contacts via voice or video

How to Spot a Deepfake

No single method is foolproof, but these signs can help you identify manipulated media:

Visual Clues

  • Unnatural blinking — Early deepfakes often had irregular or absent blinking patterns
  • Edge artifacts — Look for blurring or flickering around the hairline, jaw, and neck
  • Inconsistent lighting — Shadows or reflections that don't match the scene
  • Skin texture — Overly smooth or waxy-looking skin, especially compared to the rest of the frame
  • Teeth and ears — Often rendered poorly, with asymmetry or blurriness
  • Background warping — Straight lines or objects behind the person may distort as the face moves

Audio Clues

  • Robotic cadence — Slightly unnatural rhythm or pacing in speech
  • Breathing patterns — Absence of natural pauses, breaths, or filler sounds
  • Background noise mismatch — Ambient sound that doesn't match the claimed environment
  • Emotional flatness — The voice may lack natural emotional variation

Practical Steps to Protect Yourself

1. Verify Before You Trust

If you receive an unexpected video call, voice message, or video from someone you know — especially if they're asking for money or sensitive information — verify through a separate channel. Call them back on a known number or ask a question only they would know.

2. Establish a Code Word

Agree on a secret code word with family members and close contacts. If someone calls claiming to be a relative in distress, ask for the code word. A deepfake won't know it.

3. Limit Your Digital Footprint

The more public audio and video of you exists, the easier it is to create a convincing deepfake. Consider:

  • Restricting who can see your social media posts
  • Avoiding posting long, clear audio or video publicly
  • Being selective about which platforms have your biometric data

4. Use Deepfake Detection Tools

Several tools and services can help identify synthetic media:

  • Microsoft Video Authenticator — Analyzes photos and videos for manipulation
  • Sensity AI — Detects deepfakes in images and videos
  • Hive Moderation — API-based detection for platforms and developers
  • Reverse image search — Check if a profile photo appears elsewhere or was AI-generated

5. Check the Source

Before sharing or acting on a video:

  • Is it from a verified, reputable source?
  • Have other credible outlets reported the same thing?
  • Does the original source have a history of reliability?
  • Was it shared with urgency designed to prevent you from thinking critically?

6. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

Don't rely on voice or video verification alone for sensitive operations. Use MFA, hardware keys, or other non-biometric verification methods for financial transactions and account access.

7. Stay Informed

Deepfake technology evolves rapidly. Follow cybersecurity news and understand the latest techniques so you can recognize new attack vectors as they emerge.

What Organizations Should Do

  • Train employees to recognize deepfake-based social engineering attacks
  • Implement verification protocols for high-value transactions (e.g., callback verification for wire transfers)
  • Use content provenance standards like C2PA to verify media authenticity
  • Deploy detection tools in content moderation pipelines
  • Have an incident response plan for when deepfakes target your organization or executives

The Bottom Line

Deepfakes exploit our natural tendency to trust what we see and hear. The best defense is a healthy skepticism combined with practical verification habits. If something feels off — even slightly — verify it through a trusted, independent channel before acting on it.

Technology alone won't solve this. The most effective protection is a combination of awareness, verification habits, and the right tools.
